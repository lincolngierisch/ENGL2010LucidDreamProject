A Look Into Our Minds: The Effects of Brainwave Stimulation on Lucid Dreaming

Published on March 30, 2025 | Dream Research
Lincoln Gierisch
Department of English, Salt Lake Community College

Lucid dreaming is the event where a dreamer realizes they are dreaming. In some cases, they may gain control over the events in their dream. It's a sort of hybrid dreaming-awake state that gives us a new perspective on our own dreams. Lucid dreaming has interested psychologists for many years, and rightfully so. It's a special type of dream, and certain people get to experience it more than others, leading to a lot of research on the topic. What I have chosen to discover is a simple question: Can brainwave stimulation enhance the clarity or lucidity of dreams? Whether external or direct, brainwave stimulation does show potential for enhancing the lucidity and clarity of dreams.

Understanding Lucid Dream Induction Methods

In order to study lucid dreaming, we first need to induce these types of dreams. Traditionally, we have frequently used three methods: Reality Testing (RT), Mnemonic Induction of Lucid Dreaming (MILD), and the Wake Back To Bed (WBTB) method. These techniques rely on enhancing self-awareness and cognition during sleep.

Our brain operates through electrical oscillation, kind of similar to how AC electricity works. Just like AC electricity, these signals are measured in hertz, which is the number of cycles per second. For brain signals, however, there are multiple stages, each signifying different types of cognitive function. The slowest frequency is the delta frequency, followed by theta, then alpha, then beta, then gamma waves at the fastest. Lucid dreaming is most prominent during the highest frequency, gamma waves, while asleep.

Lucid dreaming requires quite a bit of self-awareness while one is asleep. Various types of brain stimulation, such as acoustic stimulation, gives the brain a bit of awareness. This increases the chance of performing a reality check, helping one to realize they are experiencing a dream.

Methods of Brainwave Stimulation

In order to stimulate our brain to work differently, we can use various methods. The first of these methods is auditory stimulation. This could involve any type of auditory stimulation, but it's easiest if we have an automated system that produces a consistent sound for the purpose of measuring. The specific method used in one of my sources was a ringing sound. Metacognition is another cognitive technique to help stimulate the brain. This will come in the form of the reality testing, wake-back-to-bed method, and mnemonic induction. Basically, it's where we try to think about wanting to lucid dream, and the specific methodology changes for each of the three standards. Transcranial electrical stimulation (tES) is a more "brute force" way of brainwave stimulation, as it is electrically stimulating the brain. This can be harmful if done improperly, but it has the potential to uncover some very interesting results.

Auditory Stimulation Research

In the article "Ring, ring, ring... Are you dreaming Combining acoustic stimulation and reality testing for lucid dream induction: A sleep laboratory study", the authors take a look at the effect of acoustic stimulation as a dream signal (Erlacher et al., 2020). What they found was that the ringing sound did indeed remind their participants to perform a reality check, leading them to recognize that they were experiencing a dream. Once lucidity was achieved in these dreams, some of the participants found they had greater control of the in-dream events, especially as the ringing continued at certain points in their dream. Because the ringing improved the lucidity of their dreams, it can be concluded that stimulation has a direct relationship with the lucidity of the dreams, as long as any participant has built the habit of doing reality checks often. Without the participants performing reality checks, it's hard to say whether an acoustic stimulation would trigger them to recognize they are experiencing a dream.

Brainwave Patterns and Lucid Dreaming

Brainwave stimulation has shown a lot of potential in enhancing lucid dreaming through targeting brainwave patterns, in particular the gamma frequency. This frequency is often associated with increased self-awareness and metacognition, which is good for lucid dreaming. It follows, then, that stimulating the gamma frequency in one way or another would increase the likelihood of a person experiencing a lucid dream, given that they are able to stay asleep during the stimulation. Acoustic stimulation has been shown to be effective when used in tandem with other methods. Erlacher et al (2020) have found in their study "Sounds of Lucidity: Auditory Stimulation for Triggering Reality Checks in Lucid Dream Induction" that combining an auditory cue with the classical Reality Testing method increased the frequency of subjects experiencing lucid dreams significantly. However, this auditory method of brainwave stimulation could be used with other methods, such as WBTB and MILD. Because the auditory cue stimulates the gamma frequency, helping a subject suddenly become more aware of their situation, they become more likely to remember they are in a dream or perform a task to notice they are in a dream.

Visual Stimulation Findings

In addition to auditory cues, we can also use visual stimulation for lucid dream induction. Combining cognitive training (RT, MILD, WBTB) with visual external stimuli had a similar effect to the auditory stimulation. In the article "Combining Presleep Cognitive Training and REM-Sleep Stimulation in a Laboratory Morning Nap for Lucid Dream Induction" the authors find that combining MILD techniques with targeted visual stimulation during REM sleep led to significantly increased levels of lucid dreaming when compared to the control (Carr et al. 2020). This highlights the similarities between visual and auditory stimulation when it comes to the induction of lucid dreaming. Each help to cue the participant to recognize their dream state and turn it into a lucid dream. Each of these external stimuli that influence the brain function help to show that changes in the brain's patterns of thought while in a dream state lead to a higher likelihood of the dreamer recognizing their dream state.

The Neuroscience Behind Lucid Dreaming

One promising area of research in lucid dreaming is in understanding the specific brainwave patterns that cause this unique dream state. Baird, Mota-Rolim, and Dresler (2019) demonstrate through their article "The cognitive neuroscience of lucid dreaming" that lucid dreaming notably combines theta waves (for normal REM sleep) with wake-like gamma and beta waves (13-100Hz), largely in the prefrontal cortex, which is quite unnatural for regular dreaming. This hybrid dreaming-awake state is what allows us to have metacognition and control while in the dream, since the gamma waves are particularly powerful for self-awareness and executive function. This allows logical thought to exist alongside the unnatural, strange dream experiences. This suggests that certain stimulation techniques that target gamma frequencies could be the secret to reliably inducing lucid dreams. In particular, tES could be a potential method to induce certain brainwave stimulation, as it can be fine-tuned to deliver electrical currents at specific frequencies. Right now, we don't have much research on that, but there may be a way to set up a study to test this out.

Combining Techniques for Better Results

Traditional cognitive techniques (MILD, RT, and WBTB) are foundational for lucid dream induction these days. However, using any of these individually is far less effective than the combination of the techniques. In "Reality Testing and the Mnemonic Induction of Lucid Dreams: Findings From the National Australian Lucid Dream Induction Study" by Denholm Jay Adventure-Heart et al, they claim, "The RT+WBTB+MILD condition was clearly the most effective at inducing lucid dreams." While each of these techniques has its own strengths, there are limitations to what any one of these methods can pull off. Reality testing, for example, requires the development of a habit, which makes it especially weak when the individual is skeptical about the effectiveness of RT. The Wake-Back-To-Bed method is also difficult in that it requires optimal timing, which will require trial and error. The Mnemonic induction method is one that requires a lot of concentration and practice. Each of these methods apart can be easy to forget to do, but going through with all of them means there are multiple methods to induce a lucid dream, which means that even if one doesn't work, somebody may be more reliably induced into lucid dreaming through another method.

Combining multiple techniques can significantly increase the success rate of lucid dream induction. Adventure-Heart et al. (2017) found that combining Reality Testing (RT), Mnemonic Induction of Lucid Dreaming (MILD), and Wake Back To Bed (WBTB) was a significantly more reliable method for inducing a lucid dream than any of these methods alone. RT trained a habit for recognizing awareness, MILD strengthened the ability to create mental reminders, and the WBTB method uses the sleep cycle as a way to consistently time REM sleep for recognizing occurring dreams. However, it can be more reliable to induce lucidity with another element: acoustic stimulation. Similar to RT, auditory cues act as a trigger for in-dream awareness (Erlacher et. al 2020). It might trigger a participant to perform a reality check or to utilize MILD and ask themselves if they are awake. Additionally if used with WBTB, an auditory cue could be triggered when the participant is in REM sleep. Auditory cues have synergy with each of the three methods of lucid dream induction, meaning the sudden sound helps to trigger a different pattern of brain activity, assisting in the induction of lucid dreams.

Individual Differences in Response

As amazing as it is that we've been able to consistently encourage lucid dreams, each of the techniques will affect different people in different ways. A study about acoustic stimulation and its influence on lucid dreaming found that not everybody was able to utilize the acoustic stimulation to recognize they were dreaming. Certain people were able to sleep through and not notice the stimuli, while others may have heard the sound in their dreams, but still weren't able to recognize that they were dreaming. Those who weren't able to experience many lucid dreams often didn't have much experience with lucid dreaming and/or didn't often perform reality checks, meaning they were less likely to remember to do a reality check while in their dream (Erlacher et al. 2020).

The Future of Brainwave Stimulation Research

As we have seen, brainwave "stimulation" (RT, WBTB, MILD, acoustic stimulation) serves as a powerful tool for enhancing lucid dreams. However, these methods are a very loose use of the word "stimulation". The use of tES as a way to trigger lucid dreams, as far as I can find, has not been done yet, nor has any other direct method of brainwave stimulation. Thus, we are in need of some future research to fill in this gap in our knowledge.

Transcranial electrical stimulation, or (tES) is a method of sending weak electrical currents to the head. This influences neuron firing in the brain according to the output of the tES. Using this, we can directly influence brain waves by synchronizing neural activity to the frequency of the applied current. Using this, we could potentially stimulate the brain to work at the gamma frequency during sleep. According to Thomas Reed and Roi Cohen Kadosh in their article "Transcranial electrical stimulation (tES) mechanisms and its effects on cortical excitability and connectivity", a certain type of tES, called Transcranial Alternating Current Stimulation (tACS) shows particular promise in enhancing memory, attention and mood while awake (Reed & Kadosh, 2018). We can see that tACS can directly alter brainwaves, which usually is used for stabilizing cognition and help with certain neurological conditions. However, it also has the potential to be used during REM sleep to induce a lucid dream and/or improve the lucidity of that dream.

Ethical Considerations

As with all research, there is a level of uncertainty and potential issues that come with trying to understand the relationship between direct brainwave stimulation and lucid dreams. There are a number of ethical concerns to tACS, including headaches, confusion, and even burns to participants if things aren't properly connected, as Reed and Kadosh have found in their research using tES. Additionally, every individual will have a unique response to tACS, which could cause issues with research. There is also the question of how even if it does work, tACS might create a different type of lucidity than normal lucidity, especially if tACS is continuously used while the participant is currently experiencing a dream. Right now, we have a lot of experimental discoveries going on. Perhaps it could be the key to a more interesting future, but until then, we'll have to look further into the potential of tES.

In the end, brainwave stimulation, whether through external cues, cognitive techniques, or the more direct tES, shows significant promise in enhancing the induction and lucidity of lucid dreams.

Conclusion

In the end, brainwave stimulation, whether through external cues, cognitive techniques, or the more direct tES, shows significant promise in enhancing the induction and lucidity of lucid dreams. While traditional methods like RT, WBTB, and MILD have each shown effectiveness on their own, combining these techniques with other methods of stimulation, such as acoustic or visual cues, will greatly increase the likelihood of triggering a lucid dream. Additionally, the emergence of tES has shown tons of potential in adding to this field with its direct influence on brainwave activity, meaning we may have a more reliable and consistent way to induce lucid dreams in the near future. Perhaps this future will be one that utilizes lucid dreams for advancements in clinical treatment or even technological advancements. As research continues to discover the potential of brainwave stimulation, we may be able to harness the power of lucid dreaming and revolutionize our knowledge of the mind.
